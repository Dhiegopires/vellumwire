## 1. Headline Formulas

### The Problem-Statement Headline
Name the pain directly. The reader self-selects.
- "A beautiful website that doesn't generate calls is a liability."
- "Your checkout flow is losing 68% of buyers."
- "Nobody reads your 'About' page. Here's what they want instead."

### The Outcome Headline
Promise the result, not the process.
- "Visits turn into calls."
- "From 2% to 11% conversion in 90 days."
- "A website that sells while you sleep."

### The Contrast Headline
Juxtapose what they have vs. what's possible.
- "You have traffic. You don't have conversions."
- "Pretty websites are easy. Profitable ones aren't."
- "Your competitors aren't better. They're clearer."

### The Specificity Headline
Concrete details > vague claims.
- "We rebuilt 47 service-business websites last year. 43 doubled their leads."
- "The 6-second test your homepage is failing."
- "3 lines of copy that tripled demo bookings for a $2M SaaS."

### The Question Headline
Ask what they're already thinking.
- "What happens when a prospect visits your site at 11pm?"
- "Can your website close a deal without you?"
- "Is your homepage a brochure or a salesperson?"

### Rules for All Headlines
- **Front-load meaning** — the first 5 words must pull weight
- **One idea per headline** — if you need "and", split into headline + subhead
- **Kill adverbs** — "dramatically improve" → "double" / "cut in half"
- **Concrete > abstract** — numbers, timeframes, specifics beat generalizations
- **Read it aloud** — if you stumble, rewrite

## 2. Page-Level Copy Frameworks

### PAS (Problem → Agitation → Solution)

Best for: landing pages, service pages, email sequences.

1. **Problem**: Name it clearly. "Your website gets traffic but not leads."
2. **Agitate**: Make it visceral. "Every visitor who bounces is calling your competitor instead."
3. **Solution**: Position your offer. "We rebuild service websites with product-level UX and proof-led copy."

### AIDA (Attention → Interest → Desire → Action)

Best for: ads, product pages, email campaigns.

1. **Attention**: Hook in 3 seconds. Headline, visual, or pattern interrupt.
2. **Interest**: Expand with specifics. Data, story, unexpected angle.
3. **Desire**: Make it personal. "Imagine opening your inbox Monday to 12 new quote requests."
4. **Action**: Clear, specific CTA. "Book your 15-min teardown."

### Before → After → Bridge

Best for: case studies, transformation narratives, testimonials.

1. **Before**: Describe the current painful state.
2. **After**: Paint the improved future state.
3. **Bridge**: Show how you get them there.

### The StoryBrand Framework (Donald Miller)

Map your customer as the hero:
1. A **character** (customer) has a **problem**
2. And meets a **guide** (you) who has **empathy** and **authority**
3. Who gives them a **plan**
4. And **calls them to action**
5. That helps them **avoid failure**
6. And ends in **success**

Key: You are not the hero. The customer is. You are the guide.

## 3. Brand Voice Framework

### Defining a Voice

A brand voice sits on spectrums:

| Spectrum | Left | Right |
|----------|------|-------|
| Formality | Casual, conversational | Formal, authoritative |
| Emotion | Warm, empathetic | Cool, detached |
| Complexity | Simple, plain language | Technical, sophisticated |
| Humour | Playful, witty | Serious, earnest |
| Confidence | Humble, qualified | Bold, assertive |
| Pace | Relaxed, flowing | Urgent, punchy |

**Pick your position on each spectrum** — and then make it extreme enough to be recognizable. A voice that's "kind of casual, sort of confident" is invisible.

### Voice vs. Tone

- **Voice** = personality. Doesn't change. (Who you are.)
- **Tone** = mood. Changes by context. (How you feel right now.)

Example: Brand voice is "direct, confident, dry wit". But:
- Error message tone: helpful, calm, specific
- Success message tone: brief, warm, celebratory
- Legal page tone: clear, neutral, thorough

### Voice Documentation Template

**We are**: [3-5 adjectives with definitions]
**We are not**: [3-5 anti-patterns with examples]
**We sound like**: [2-3 reference voices — real people, brands, publications]
**We never**: [Hard rules — words/phrases/tones that are banned]

## 4. Micro-Copy Patterns

### Form Labels
- Be specific: "Work email" not "Email"
- Add context: "Phone (we'll text your booking confirmation)"
- Never placeholder-only: labels must persist above the field

### Error Messages
Structure: **What went wrong** + **How to fix it**
- "Email needs an @ symbol — try name@company.com"
- "Password needs at least 8 characters — you're at 5"
- "This email is already registered. Sign in instead?"

### Empty States
Structure: **What this is** + **Why it's empty** + **Action to fill it**
- "No projects yet. Create your first project to get started."
- "Your inbox is clear. Nice work."

### Loading States
- Keep it brief: "Loading your dashboard..." or just a skeleton
- Never: "Please wait while we process your request" (too formal, too long)

### Success Messages
- Confirm what happened: "Booking confirmed for Thursday, 2pm"
- Show next step: "Check your email for the calendar invite"
- Tone: match the significance (routine = subtle, milestone = celebratory)

### Tooltip / Help Text
- Answer one question: "What is this?"
- Maximum 15 words
- Don't explain the obvious — only clarify the ambiguous

### Button Labels
The button should complete: "I want to ___"
- "Book a call" ✓ (I want to book a call)
- "Submit" ✗ (I want to... submit?)
- "Get my free audit" ✓
- "Learn more" ✗ (weak, vague — more about what?)

### Confirmation Dialogs
- State the action: "Delete this project?"
- State the consequence: "This will permanently remove all files and data."
- Clear button labels: "Delete project" / "Keep project" (not "OK" / "Cancel")

## 5. CTA Optimization

### Primary CTA Formula
**Specific verb** + **object** + **time/effort signal**

- "Book a 15-min teardown" → specific action, low time commitment
- "Start your free trial — no card needed" → removes the #1 objection
- "Get your audit report in 24h" → clear deliverable + timeline

### Secondary CTA Formula
**Low commitment** + **curiosity**

- "See how it works"
- "View pricing"
- "Read the case study"

### CTA Placement Rules
1. Above the fold — always. The first CTA should be visible without scrolling.
2. After value proposition — once they understand the offer.
3. After social proof — once they trust you.
4. End of page — final chance before they leave.
5. **Never** two primary CTAs competing in the same viewport.

### CTA Visual Hierarchy
- Primary: filled button, accent colour, largest
- Secondary: ghost/outline button, muted, smaller
- Tertiary: text link with arrow, smallest
- Never more than 1 primary + 1 secondary per section

## 6. Storytelling Structure

### For Case Studies
1. **Client context** — who they are, what they do (1-2 sentences)
2. **The problem** — specific, measurable (not vague "challenges")
3. **What we did** — process, not features
4. **The result** — numbers first, then qualitative impact
5. **Client quote** — in their words, not yours

### For About Pages
Don't write a biography. Write a belief statement.
1. What you believe about your industry (the thesis)
2. Why most solutions fail (the diagnosis)
3. What you do differently (the approach)
4. What this means for the client (the outcome)

### For Service Pages
1. **Name the pain** — what's broken?
2. **Show you understand** — describe their situation (proves empathy)
3. **Introduce the solution** — what you do, in outcome terms
4. **How it works** — 3-4 steps, simple
5. **Who it's for** — qualify the audience
6. **What they get** — deliverables, timeline, what changes
7. **Social proof** — relevant to this specific service
8. **CTA** — specific to this service

## 7. Editing Checklist

Run every piece of copy through these filters:

### Cut
- [ ] Remove "that" (unnecessary 80% of the time)
- [ ] Remove "very", "really", "actually", "basically", "literally"
- [ ] Remove "in order to" → "to"
- [ ] Remove "due to the fact that" → "because"
- [ ] Remove "at this point in time" → "now"
- [ ] Kill passive voice: "Mistakes were made" → "We made mistakes"
- [ ] Remove throat-clearing: "It's worth noting that..." → just say the thing

### Sharpen
- [ ] Replace vague nouns: "things", "stuff", "solutions" → specifics
- [ ] Replace vague verbs: "utilize" → "use", "leverage" → "use", "facilitate" → "help"
- [ ] Replace jargon with plain language (unless audience is technical)
- [ ] Numbers > words: "hundreds" → "347"
- [ ] Timeframes > vague timing: "soon" → "within 48 hours"

### Test
- [ ] Read aloud — if you stumble, rewrite
- [ ] The "so what?" test — after every sentence, ask "so what?" If you can't answer, cut or rewrite
- [ ] The "who cares?" test — does the reader care, or just the writer?
- [ ] First-5-word test — are the first 5 words of every paragraph strong?

## 8. Tone Calibration

### By Page Type

| Page | Tone | Characteristics |
|------|------|-----------------|
| Homepage | Confident, concise | Bold headline, sparse copy, heavy proof |
| Service page | Empathetic, then authoritative | Understand pain → show expertise |
| About page | Human, opinionated | Show beliefs, not just biography |
| Pricing | Clear, transparent | No jargon, no hidden info, comparison-friendly |
| Blog | Informative, specific | Teach don't preach, show don't tell |
| Contact | Warm, low-pressure | Remove anxiety, set expectations |
| Error/404 | Helpful, brief, light | Acknowledge the issue, provide next steps |
| Onboarding | Encouraging, patient | Celebrate progress, guide clearly |

### By Emotional Context

| User Feeling | Your Tone | Example |
|--------------|-----------|---------|
| Frustrated | Calm, specific | "This didn't work. Here's exactly how to fix it." |
| Excited | Match energy, guide | "Great choice! Here's what happens next." |
| Confused | Simple, patient | "Let's break this down. First..." |
| Skeptical | Evidence-led, humble | "Here's what we've seen with 47 clients..." |
| Anxious | Reassuring, clear | "No commitment required. Cancel anytime." |

## 9. A/B Test Copy Strategies

### What to Test (highest impact first)

1. **Headline** — the #1 lever for conversion
2. **CTA button text** — specific vs. generic
3. **Social proof placement** — before vs. after the CTA
4. **Subheadline** — benefit-led vs. feature-led
5. **Objection handling** — presence vs. absence near CTA
6. **Form length** — fewer fields vs. more fields

### Test Principles

- Change ONE element per test (unless you're testing entire page variations)
- Statistical significance before declaring a winner (minimum ~1000 visitors per variant)
- Test for 2+ full business cycles (minimum 2 weeks)
- Document: what changed, hypothesis, result, lift %

## 10. Copy for Specific Components

### Hero Section
```
[Eyebrow — topic/category, mono, small, accented]
[Headline — problem or outcome, display, large]
[Subhead — qualify audience or expand headline, body, medium]
[CTA row — primary + secondary]
[Trust signal — "Trusted by X" or "No credit card required"]
```

### Testimonial
```
"[Specific result or transformation — not generic praise]"
— [Name], [Role] at [Company]
```
Bad: "Great company, would recommend!"
Good: "Our lead volume doubled in 60 days. The audit alone was worth 10x the cost."

### FAQ
- Question: written in the customer's voice, not yours
- Answer: direct first sentence, then explain. Start with "Yes", "No", or the answer — never "Great question!"

### 404 Page
- Acknowledge: "This page doesn't exist."
- Help: "Try searching, or head back to the homepage."
- Tone: light, not dramatic. Optional: one line of personality.

### Newsletter Signup
- What they get: "Weekly teardowns of real websites — what works, what leaks."
- Frequency: "Every Thursday. 3-minute read."
- Trust: "No spam. Unsubscribe in one click."
